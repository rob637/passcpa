Hey everyone, and welcome! Are you prepping for the SEE-suh exam? ... I know it can be daunting. One of the areas that often trips people up, even experienced auditors, is system modeling. We’re going to break it down today, so you don’t fall into those same traps.

So, what is system modeling? ... In essence, it's a representation of a system, built to understand its behavior, structure, or function. We're talking about creating diagrams, charts, or descriptions that simplify complex systems. ... Key terms here are: data flow diagrams (DFDs), entity-relationship diagrams (ERDs), Unified Modeling Language (UML), and business process models (BPMN). Don't get bogged down in memorizing every symbol, but understand their purpose.

...

Why does this matter for auditors? ... Well, as auditors, we need to understand the systems we're auditing! We need to assess risks and controls. System models provide a visual, simplified view of complex processes, helping us identify vulnerabilities and inefficiencies. Think about it: if you’re auditing a loan application system, a BPMN diagram could quickly reveal bottlenecks or points where segregation of duties is weak. Similarly, an ERD can expose data integrity issues. ... It's about understanding the flow and the relationships within the system. Without this understanding, you're essentially auditing in the dark.

...

Now, let’s talk about the exam traps. ... First, watch out for questions that focus exclusively on the technical aspects of a modeling notation. The SEE-suh exam is about auditing, not systems development. You don't need to be a UML expert. Focus on how the model aids in the audit process.

...

Second, don't confuse process modeling with data modeling. A BPMN diagram describes how something is done, the sequence of activities. An ERD describes what data is stored and the relationships between different pieces of data. The exam will try to trick you by presenting scenarios where the wrong type of model is suggested. ... Pay close attention to what the question is asking about.

...

Third, the answer that looks right, but isn't, often involves choosing the most detailed model. Remember, the goal is to simplify and understand. An overly complex model defeats the purpose. ... Look for the model that provides the necessary information for audit purposes, without being overwhelming.

...

Fourth, be wary of answers that suggest system models are only useful during the initial audit. System models are valuable for ongoing monitoring and risk assessment. They can help you track changes to the system and identify potential control weaknesses over time. They're not a "one and done" activity.

...

Let’s dive into some examples. ... Let’s say you're auditing a company that's implemented a new e-commerce platform. The question asks you to identify the most appropriate system model to assess the flow of customer orders from placement to fulfillment. ... Would you choose an ERD, a DFD, or a UML sequence diagram?

...

Here's the breakdown: An ERD would show the database structure, which is useful, but doesn't directly illustrate the order flow. A UML sequence diagram could show the flow, but might be overly technical for an initial audit assessment. A DFD, on the other hand, would clearly depict the movement of data related to the order, from the customer interface, through the various processing stages, to fulfillment and payment. Therefore, a DFD is likely the best answer in this scenario.

...

Another example: Suppose you're auditing a healthcare provider and need to understand how patient data is managed and related across different systems (e.g., billing, electronic health records). In this case, an ERD would be extremely valuable. It would visually represent the entities (patients, doctors, appointments, etc.) and the relationships between them. This can help you assess data integrity, security, and compliance with regulations like HIP-ah.

...

   Diagram: They often involve diagrams.
   Identify: They help identify risks.
   Track: They help track changes.
   ABLE: Makes you ABLE to audit the system effectively.

Think of it like a map. ... A system model is a map of a complex system, allowing you to navigate it more effectively and identify potential hazards.

Second, focus on how these models aid the audit process, not on the technical details of the notation. Third, system models are valuable throughout the audit lifecycle, not just during initial assessments.
