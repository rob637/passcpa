## FAR-05: Cash Flow Statements — The Three-Minute Method
**Style: "Exam Day Speed Technique"**

```
[AI INSTRUCTIONS - DO NOT SPEAK THIS]
Format: YouTube Shorts (9:16 vertical)
Duration: 55 seconds
Visuals: Show timer ticking. Quick visual of statement of cash flows structure. Animate adjustments as +/- indicators. "Operating" section as the hero. Show balance sheet changes converting to cash flow adjustments.
Music: Upbeat, "let's move" energy. Clock ticking subtly in background.
Text overlays: RED for subtract, GREEN for add. Show "Asset UP = subtract" type rules clearly.
```

```
[SPOKEN SCRIPT - VOICE READS THIS EXACTLY]
Cash flow statements show up on almost every CPA Exam. Here's how to build the operating section in under three minutes.

Start with net income. Now you're going to adjust for everything that wasn't cash.

First adjustment — depreciation and amortization. Those reduced net income but no cash left. Add them back.

Second — gains and losses. A gain on selling equipment? That's in investing activities, not operating. Subtract gains, add losses.

Third — working capital changes. This is where people mess up. Here's the rule: 

If a current ASSET went UP, subtract. You spent cash to build that asset.
If a current ASSET went DOWN, add. You collected cash.
If a current LIABILITY went UP, add. You owe more but didn't pay cash.
If a current LIABILITY went DOWN, subtract. You used cash to pay it off.

Asset up, subtract. Asset down, add. Liability up, add. Liability down, subtract.

Start with net income. Adjust for non-cash. Adjust for working capital. Done.
```

```
Tracing or vouching. The CPA Exam LOVES to flip these. Here's how to never confuse them.

Picture an elevator with three floors. Ground floor is source documents. Middle floor is the journal. Top floor is the ledger.

Tracing goes UP. Start at a source document and trace it up to make sure it got recorded. You're testing completeness. Did everything get INTO the books?
```

**Style: "The Mistake Everyone Makes" Warning**

```
[AI INSTRUCTIONS - DO NOT SPEAK THIS]
Format: YouTube Shorts (9:16 vertical)
Duration: 55 seconds
Visuals: Split screen — S-Corp shareholder on left hitting a wall, Partner on right climbing a ladder. Wall labeled "NO DEBT BASIS." Ladder labeled "DEBT = MORE BASIS." Warning signs and comparison graphics.
Music: Slightly tense, cautionary tone. Resolution at the comparison.
Text overlays: "S-CORP: No entity debt basis" vs "PARTNERSHIP: Entity debt adds to basis" in clear comparison.
```

```
[SPOKEN SCRIPT - VOICE READS THIS EXACTLY]
The number one CPA Exam trap on REG: basis rules for S-Corps versus partnerships. Get this wrong, and the whole question is wrong.

Here's the difference that kills people.

In an S-Corp, shareholders generally get NO basis for entity debt. The company can borrow ten million dollars, and your basis? Doesn't change. The only way to get debt basis in an S-Corp is if YOU personally loan money to the company.

Partnerships are completely different. Partners get basis for entity debt. The partnership borrows money, and your basis goes UP. Recourse debt, nonrecourse debt, qualified nonrecourse financing — it all adds to your basis.

Why does this matter? Because basis limits your deductions. No basis, no loss deduction.

So the S-Corp shareholder hits a wall. They can't deduct losses beyond their stock basis plus direct loans.

The partner climbs a ladder. Entity debt gives them more basis to deduct against.

S-Corp, wall. Partnership, ladder.

That distinction is worth multiple points on the CPA Exam. Don't miss it.
```

```
CPA Exam business law scenario. An employee signs a contract on behalf of the company. Is the boss liable?

It depends on one thing: authority.

Actual authority is real. The boss gave it. Either expressly — "you can sign contracts up to fifty thousand dollars" — or impliedly — if you're a manager, you probably have authority to do manager things.

Actual authority comes from the principal. Apparent authority comes from what the third party reasonably believes based on the principal's conduct.

Know both. The CPA Exam tests both.
```

```
[SPOKEN SCRIPT - VOICE READS THIS EXACTLY]
Cost accounting variance analysis on the CPA BAR exam. Two formulas. Here's how to understand them instead of memorize them.

Variance analysis answers one question: why did actual cost differ from what we expected?

There are only two reasons. Either the PRICE was different. Or the QUANTITY was different.

Price variance isolates the price factor. You hold quantity constant at actuals and ask: what's the price difference? Actual price minus standard price, times actual quantity.

Usage variance isolates the quantity factor. You hold price constant at standards and ask: what's the quantity difference? Actual quantity minus standard quantity, times standard price.

Notice the pattern. Price variance uses actual quantity. Usage variance uses standard price. You're always holding one factor constant to isolate the other.

If actual is higher than standard, that's unfavorable — you spent more. If actual is lower, that's favorable — you saved.

Two formulas. Two factors. Isolate one, hold the other constant.
```

```
[SPOKEN SCRIPT - VOICE READS THIS EXACTLY]
CPA TCP exam. Your client sells investment property for a huge gain. Tax bill could be six figures. But there's a way to defer it — maybe forever.

Section ten thirty-one. The like-kind exchange.

Here's how it works. Instead of selling and pocketing the cash, you exchange the property for another qualifying property. Real property for real property. The gain isn't recognized — it's deferred. Your basis carries over to the new property.

But there's a catch. If you receive "boot" — anything that's not like-kind property — that part is taxable.

Boot includes cash, mortgage relief, or personal property thrown into the deal. If you receive any boot, you recognize gain. But only up to the boot amount, and never more than your actual realized gain.

The formula: recognized gain equals the lesser of realized gain or boot received.

Like-kind property only? Full deferral. Any boot? Taxable to that extent.

Sophisticated investors use ten thirty-one exchanges for decades. The CPA Exam expects you to know the rules.
```
